ProtSCAPE:

Mapping the landscape of protein conformations

in molecular dynamics

Abstract

Proteins are inherently dynamic molecules whose biological functions depend on transitions among multiple conformational states. Although recent machine learning methods have achieved remarkable success in predicting static protein structures, accurately modeling conformational ensembles and time-dependent dynamics remains a major challenge. Molecular dynamics (MD) simulations provide atomistic trajectories of protein motion, but generating sufficiently long and densely sampled trajectories is computationally expensive. In this work, we introduce and evaluate ProtSCAPE, a geometric deep learning framework that learns latent representations of protein conformations and generates new structures through diffusion-based sampling in its latent space, as a method for reconstructing dense ensembles from sparsely sampled MD data.

We study an upsampling setting in which ProtSCAPE is trained on only every tenth frame of reference MD trajectories and tasked with generating full ensembles for five benchmark protein systems. Performance is assessed using both structural quality metrics, including MolProbity score, clash score, and Ramachandran outliers, and ensemble-level agreement metrics, including RMSF correlation, pairwise RMSD, Root Mean Wasserstein Distance (RMWD), and PCA-space Wasserstein distances. Across all systems, ProtSCAPE consistently produces stereochemically plausible structures while closely matching the equilibrium distributions and collective motions of the original MD simulations. In many cases, it outperforms competing generative baselines including AlphaFLOW and MDGen.

These results demonstrate that ProtSCAPE can efficiently recover realistic protein conformational landscapes from limited trajectory data, highlighting the promise of latent generative models for scalable analysis and acceleration of biomolecular simulation.
